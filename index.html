Career & ATS Guides

How to Beat the ATS in 2025

Learn proven strategies to make your resume stand out and pass Applicant Tracking Systems with our comprehensive guide.

Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS) are software applications used by employers to manage recruitment processes. They scan resumes for keywords, formatting, and relevance before a human ever sees them. To beat them in 2025:

1. Keyword Optimization: Carefully analyze job descriptions and incorporate relevant keywords naturally throughout your resume. Focus on both hard skills (Python, React, AWS) and soft skills (leadership, communication).

2. Clean Formatting: Use simple, standard fonts like Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman. Avoid tables, headers/footers, graphics, and columns that can confuse ATS parsers.

3. Standard Section Headings: Use conventional headings like "Work Experience," "Education," and "Skills" rather than creative alternatives.

4. Quantifiable Achievements: Include numbers and metrics wherever possible (e.g., "Increased sales by 25%," "Reduced costs by $50,000").

5. File Format: Submit your resume as a .docx or .pdf file unless otherwise specified. Some older ATS systems struggle with .pdf files.

In 2025, ATS systems are becoming more sophisticated with AI-based parsing, so staying updated with current best practices is essential.

Top 10 Resume Mistakes to Avoid

Common mistakes include keyword stuffing, fancy graphics, missing metrics, and vague job descriptions.

ATS systems reward clarity and relevance, not design gimmicks. Here are the top 10 mistakes job seekers make:

1. Keyword Stuffing: Overloading your resume with keywords in an unnatural way can trigger ATS spam filters.

2. Fancy Graphics and Tables: While visually appealing to humans, these often confuse ATS systems, causing them to misparse your content.

3. Missing Contact Information: Surprisingly common - ensure your phone, email, and LinkedIn profile are clearly listed.

4. Vague Job Descriptions: Instead of "Responsible for sales," write "Increased regional sales by 30% through new client acquisition strategies."

5. Incorrect File Format: Some ATS systems can't read .pdf files properly, especially older versions. When in doubt, use .docx.

6. Spelling and Grammar Errors: Many ATS systems now flag resumes with numerous errors as low-quality.

7. Including Photos: Unnecessary and can cause ATS parsing issues in some regions.

8. Using Uncommon Fonts: Stick to standard, easily readable fonts to ensure proper parsing.

9. Too Long or Too Short: For most professionals, 1-2 pages is ideal. Recent graduates should aim for one page.

10. Not Customizing for Each Job: Sending the same generic resume to every job application significantly reduces your chances.

Perfect Resume Formatting Guide

Learn the best formatting practices for modern resumes that get results with both ATS and human readers.

Proper formatting is crucial for both ATS compatibility and human readability. Follow these guidelines:

Font and Size: Use 10-12pt font for body text, 14-16pt for your name. Recommended fonts: Arial, Calibri, Georgia, Times New Roman, or Helvetica.

Margins: Set margins to 0.5-1 inch on all sides. Too-small margins can make your resume look crowded; too-large margins waste valuable space.

Section Organization: Follow this standard order: Contact Information, Professional Summary, Work Experience, Education, Skills, Certifications (optional).

Bullet Points: Use bullet points (‚Ä¢) rather than asterisks or other symbols. Start each bullet with a strong action verb (Managed, Developed, Increased, etc.).

Length: For most professionals with less than 10 years of experience, one page is ideal. For senior professionals, two pages is acceptable.

White Space: Ensure adequate white space between sections for readability. Use consistent spacing throughout.

File Naming: Name your file professionally: "FirstName_LastName_Resume.pdf" or "FirstName_LastName_TargetJob_Resume.pdf"

Writing Effective Cover Letters

How to write cover letters that complement your resume and significantly increase your interview chances.

While some claim cover letters are obsolete, our data shows they can increase interview rates by up to 30% when done well. Here's how to write effective ones:

1. Address It Properly: Whenever possible, address your cover letter to a specific person. "Dear Hiring Manager" is acceptable when you can't find a name.

2. First Paragraph - The Hook: Immediately state the position you're applying for and why you're excited about it. Mention a specific aspect of the company that appeals to you.

3. Second Paragraph - The Connection: Connect your most relevant experience to the job requirements. Don't just repeat your resume - explain how your experience applies.

4. Third Paragraph - The Value: Explain what unique value you can bring to the company. Mention specific skills or experiences that make you stand out.

5. Closing Paragraph - The Call to Action: Thank them for their consideration, express enthusiasm for discussing your qualifications further, and mention any attachments.

Length: Keep it to one page, or about 250-400 words. Hiring managers spend an average of 7 seconds on initial cover letter review.

ATS Considerations: Like resumes, cover letters should use simple formatting and include relevant keywords from the job description.
